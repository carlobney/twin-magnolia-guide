Louisiana Cottage Food Law

Louisiana Act 542 (2014) allows home-based production of certain foods without a commercial kitchen.

What's Allowed

  • Baked goods: breads, cakes, cookies, pies
  • Candies and confections
  • Dried mixes
  • Jams, jellies, preserves
  • Honey and honeycomb products
  • Pickles and acidified foods (pH below 4.6)
  • Sauces and syrups
  • Spices
Good news: Custard and cream-filled items ARE allowed in Louisiana (unlike most states) ‚Äî just use pasteurized milk products and follow temp requirements.

Restrictions for Breads/Cakes/Cookies/Pies

  • Cannot employ anyone to help with production
  • No pets allowed in preparation area ‚Äî ever
  • Refrigerator must maintain 45¬∞F or below
  • Cannot sell for resale (direct to consumer only)

Cold Brew Coffee ‚Äî What You Need

Coffee is NOT a cottage food. Even cold brew (no electricity, served cold) requires food service permitting because it's a beverage prepared for public consumption.

Options for Coffee Service

Option A: Mobile Food Establishment

Get permitted as a mobile food vendor with LDH.

  • Requires approved commissary (shared commercial kitchen for prep/storage)
  • Plans review: $100 standard / $1,000 expedited
  • Annual permit fee: varies by parish
  • Food Safety Certification required

Best for: Full coffee service with hot options later

Option B: Temporary Event Permits

Get temporary permits for each event/market.

  • Per-event permits from parish health dept
  • Lower upfront cost
  • More paperwork per event
  • May have limitations on frequency

Best for: Testing the market, fewer events

Cold Brew Specifics

Cold brew concentrate is typically considered a Time/Temperature Control for Safety (TCS) food because:

  • It's a low-acid beverage (pH above 4.6)
  • Requires refrigeration at 41¬∞F or below
  • Has a limited shelf life (typically 7-14 days refrigerated)

The Commissary Requirement ‚Äî And How to Avoid It

The commissary requirement is often the biggest hurdle for small mobile vendors. Here's what you need to know.

What Is a Commissary?

A commissary is a licensed commercial kitchen facility that mobile food vendors must have a formal agreement with. It provides:

  • Food preparation space ‚Äî Licensed, inspected kitchen for prep work
  • Equipment storage ‚Äî Secure place to store your gear overnight
  • Equipment cleaning/sanitizing ‚Äî Commercial 3-compartment sink or dishwasher
  • Potable water supply ‚Äî For filling containers
  • Wastewater disposal ‚Äî Proper grease trap and drain access
  • Dry goods & refrigerated storage ‚Äî For ingredients between events
The Problem: Finding and paying for commissary access can cost $200-800/month, which destroys the economics of a small event-based business doing 5-10 gallons of cold brew per week.

Why Cold Brew Is Different

The commissary requirement was designed for food trucks doing actual cooking. Cold brew is fundamentally simpler.

üçî What Commissary Rules Target

  • Raw meat handling & cooking
  • Hot holding equipment
  • Grease and cooking oil disposal
  • Complex multi-ingredient prep
  • High-volume daily operations
  • Equipment that needs commercial cleaning

‚òï What Cold Brew Actually Is

  • Coffee grounds + filtered water
  • Steep 12-24 hours in refrigerator
  • Strain through filter
  • Keep cold in sealed containers
  • No heat, no cooking
  • No grease, no complex equipment
The Argument: Cold brew preparation is no more complex than making iced tea at home. It requires only a refrigerator, food-safe containers, and basic filtering ‚Äî all of which exist in a residential kitchen that meets cottage food standards.

Exact Questions to Ask LDH

When you call, be specific. Get answers in writing (email follow-up). Here's your script:

üìû The Call Script

"Hi, I'm starting a small event-based business selling cold brew coffee at farmers markets and festivals in [Parish]. I have some specific questions about permitting requirements."

  1. "Does cold brew coffee prepared at home and sold at events require a commissary agreement?"
    Listen for: exemptions for "limited preparation," "low-risk beverages," or "non-TCS items"
  2. "Is there a 'limited food preparation' or 'pre-packaged beverages' exemption that would apply?"
    Some states allow this for items that don't require on-site cooking
  3. "Can I operate under temporary event permits without a commissary if I'm only doing cold brew ‚Äî no cooking, no hot beverages?"
    Temporary permits often have different (lighter) requirements
  4. "What if I sell sealed, pre-bottled cold brew rather than pouring from a dispenser?"
    Pre-packaged may fall under different rules than "food service"
  5. "Are there different requirements for operations under $30,000/year in sales?"
    The cottage food sales threshold might unlock lighter regs elsewhere
Document everything. After the call, send an email: "Per our conversation today, I understand that [X]. Please confirm this is correct." Get it in writing.

The Pre-Bottled Cold Brew Strategy

Selling sealed bottles instead of pouring drinks may change your regulatory category entirely.

Key distinction: "Food service" (pouring drinks) vs. "Food manufacturing" (selling sealed products) fall under different rules. Pre-bottled might actually be easier to permit.

How It Would Work

  1. Brew at home ‚Äî Same process: coffee + water, steep, strain
  2. Bottle in sealed containers ‚Äî Glass bottles or sealed plastic with tamper-evident caps
  3. Label properly ‚Äî Product name, ingredients, your business info, "Keep Refrigerated"
  4. Keep cold at events ‚Äî Cooler with ice, temp log
  5. Sell sealed bottles ‚Äî Customer takes home, opens themselves

Questions to Ask LDH About This Approach

  • "If I sell only sealed, pre-bottled cold brew (not poured drinks), what permits apply?"
  • "Does pre-packaged cold brew fall under retail food service or food manufacturing?"
  • "What labeling is required for bottled cold brew sold direct to consumers?"
Trade-off: Pre-bottled means no customization (no adding milk/cream on-site), but it might eliminate the commissary requirement entirely. Worth exploring.

Bonus: The Cottage Food Angle

Louisiana cottage food law allows "sauces and syrups." Cold brew concentrate could arguably fall into this category ‚Äî it's essentially coffee syrup that customers dilute at home. This is a stretch, but worth asking LDH directly.

Dairy/Non-Dairy Separation

Managing allergens is critical for safety and liability.

Separate Production

  • Dedicate specific days or times for dairy-free production
  • Use separate mixing bowls, utensils, and baking pans
  • Thoroughly clean all surfaces between batches
  • Store dairy and non-dairy ingredients separately

Labeling Protocol

  • Clearly mark "DAIRY FREE" on non-dairy products
  • List all allergens on every label
  • Consider color-coded labels (green = dairy-free)
  • Include "Made in a facility that also processes dairy"
Documentation: Keep a production log showing when dairy vs. non-dairy batches were made. This protects you in case of any claims and demonstrates due diligence.

For Cold Brew

Offer dairy-free milk alternatives (oat, almond, coconut) with clearly labeled options. Use separate pitchers for each type.
